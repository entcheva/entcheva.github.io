Data Structures - Binary Trees

2017-01-24

During the junior developer panel at Flatiron School, when asked about typical interview questions, one of the panelists mentioned getting the task to:

“write a function that gets the depth of a tree”

I hear and read about trees a lot and I don’t really know what they are, so I thought this was a good opportunity to get into it a little bit and at least find out what a tree is.

On page 47 (10% into the book) of Cracking the Coding Interview, Gayle Laakmann McDowell outlines the “absolute must have basics” of data structures and algorithms to know for an interview:

I want to qualify this post by mentioning that while this book is really useful and comprehensive, it is seemingly geared toward the “Stanford Big Four” set - kids who graduated from Stanford and are vying to work at Google, Facebook, Apple, or Microsoft. That’s not what I’m interested in, but in any case it’s an interesting book, and it’s good to know what some other parts of the tech industry are expecting and/or bringing to the table. And as we heard in the junior developer panel, it is likely that you will be asked some sort of CS questions during the interview process.

McDowell mentions the above as being the absolute basics of CS concepts to know - I also think it’s important to note that CS grads can also swing the other way, with lots of theoretical knowledge and no practical experience. I personally know CS grads who had a hard time in the job hunt process because of their lack of practical experience.

Anyway, these are all qualifiers to clarify that we should not feel bad that we don’t know McDowell’s “absolute basics” and not feel like we have to compare ourselves in any way - the only goal is greater knowledge.

A general tree is a data structure in that each node can have infinite number of children, A binary tree is a data structure in that each node has at most two nodes left and right. There is no limit on the degree of node in a general tree. Nodes in a binary tree cannot have more than degree 2
